\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Finding transition states (TSs) --- index-1 saddle points on the potential
energy surface --- requires second-order (Hessian) information for reliable
convergence, yet the cost of \emph{ab initio} Hessians has driven the field to
avoid them: through iterative diagonalization, quasi-Newton (QN) history
updates, or, most recently, machine-learned prediction of the Hessian or its
leftmost eigenvector. We argue that machine-learned interatomic potentials
(MLIPs) change the premise. When an analytic Hessian costs a fraction of a
second, the binding constraint is no longer the Hessian but the \emph{step}:
how to convert curvature into a geometry update that survives a poor initial
guess. We show that the simplest possible answer --- a first-order Gentlest
Ascent Dynamics (GAD) walk along the lowest eigenvector of the
Eckart-projected analytic Hessian --- is markedly more robust than
trust-region/Newton stepping as the initial guess degrades. Our primary
measure of success is the \emph{intended success rate}: after optimization we
follow the intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) forward and backward and require
both endpoints to recover the intended reactant and product by molecular-graph
isomorphism. On 287 reactions from the Transition1x test set, evaluated with the
HIP MLIP under a controlled Gaussian perturbation of the saddle geometry from 10
to \SI{200}{pm}, GAD matches the strongest Sella baseline at low noise and pulls
decisively ahead at high noise, improving the intended success rate by
\textbf{+21.3 percentage points at \SI{200}{pm}} (44.6\% vs.\ 23.3\%). The
mechanism is geometric: small Euler steps walk through basins, whereas
Newton/RFO jumps cross basin boundaries and converge to unintended saddles. % TODO: REWRITE — unintended~=0 for all methods; real mechanism is recover-both-channels vs partial/non-convergence. Also frame +21.3@200pm as the endpoint of a trend (significant 150-200pm), not the headline number.
We
then characterize the one regime where first-order dynamics is fundamentally
limited --- a force-convergence (\fmax) plateau near \SI{0.01}{eV/\AA} that no
step budget removes --- and show that a GAD$\rightarrow$Newton hybrid eliminates
it, simultaneously winning wall-time per converged TS (up to $3.0\times$ vs.\
Sella) and producing the geometrically tightest structures ($7.7\times$ tighter
95th-percentile RMSD at \SI{200}{pm}). The result is a TS finder whose
robustness, cost, and accuracy all improve precisely where existing optimizers
fail: when the starting structure is far from the saddle.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================

Transition states are the geometric bottlenecks of chemical reactions: index-1
saddle points whose energy sets the kinetic barrier and whose structure encodes
the mechanism. Locating them is harder than locating minima because the
optimizer must \emph{ascend} one direction (the reaction coordinate) while
descending all others. Doing this reliably requires curvature information ---
the Hessian, or at least its leftmost (most negative) eigenpair, which defines
the ascent direction.

The difficulty is that \emph{ab initio} Hessians are expensive, so essentially
every practical TS optimizer is built to avoid computing them. Quasi-Newton
methods accumulate an approximate Hessian from gradient history; the dimer
method follows the minimum-mode using only first derivatives \cite{dimer};
eigenvector-following and (restricted-step) partitioned rational-function
optimization (P-RFO) recover only the leftmost eigenpair on demand
\cite{cerjanmiller,banerjee,baker}; the Sella optimizer does this by iterative
partial-Hessian diagonalization in redundant internal coordinates
\cite{sella2019,sella2022}. A recent wave of machine learning predicts the
Hessian directly \cite{newtonnet,hip}, or predicts just its leftmost eigenvector
\cite{lmhe}, to sidestep the cost entirely. All of these answer the same
question: \emph{how do we get curvature without paying for the Hessian?}

\paragraph{The premise has changed.} Machine-learned interatomic potentials now
deliver analytic energies, forces, \emph{and} Hessians by automatic
differentiation or by direct prediction at a cost of order \SI{0.1}{s} per
structure; on the systems studied here, the HIP potential \cite{hip} returns a
full analytic Hessian in $\approx\!\SI{0.06}{s}$. Once curvature is cheap, the
question above dissolves: we need not approximate, diagonalize, or predict the
Hessian --- we can compute the exact one at every step. What then becomes the
bottleneck is the \textbf{step}, the rule that turns a gradient and a Hessian
into a geometry update. And here the conventional choice is questionable.
Trust-region Newton and RFO take large ($\mathcal{O}(0.1\,\text{\AA})$)
quasi-Newton jumps that are optimal near a quadratic saddle but, from a degraded
initial guess, can jump across a basin boundary and converge confidently to an
unintended saddle.

\paragraph{This work.} We take the opposite, deliberately minimal approach.
Given a cheap exact Hessian, we discard P-RFO, trust radii, QN updates,
eigenvector prediction, and ensembling, and step with first-order Gentlest
Ascent Dynamics (GAD) \cite{gad}: reflect the force about the lowest Hessian
eigenvector and take a small Euler step. The simplicity is not a compromise; it
is the source of the method's robustness. We evaluate on the Transition1x
benchmark \cite{transition1x} and, following the convention of the recent
ML-Hessian TS literature \cite{newtonnet,lmhe}, take as our \emph{primary}
metric the IRC-validated \emph{intended success rate} rather than the weaker
question of whether the optimizer merely reached a saddle. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em, itemsep=0.15em]
\item \textbf{GAD as a robust TS finder.} A first-order GAD walk on the lowest
  eigenvector of the \emph{Eckart-projected} analytic Hessian, with no
  second-order stepping machinery (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).
\item \textbf{A controlled robustness benchmark, scored by intended success.} A
  six-level Gaussian perturbation of the true saddle (10--\SI{200}{pm}) on 287
  Transition1x reactions, scored by IRC-endpoint graph matching into the
  standard \emph{intended / partially intended / unintended} taxonomy
  (Section~\ref{sec:robust}).
\item \textbf{The \fmax{} plateau.} A mechanistic, quantitatively confirmed
  limit of first-order saddle dynamics: GAD cannot tighten the maximum force
  below $\approx\!\SI{0.01}{eV/\AA}$, and no step budget removes it
  (Section~\ref{sec:fmax}).
\item \textbf{A hybrid that removes the plateau.} A GAD$\rightarrow$Newton
  switch that retains GAD's in-basin robustness while winning wall-time and
  producing the tightest geometries (Section~\ref{sec:hybrid}).
\end{enumerate}

The thesis throughout is a single sentence: \emph{in the MLIP regime, you do not
need to avoid the Hessian --- you need to step more carefully with it.}

% =====================================================================
\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Gentlest Ascent Dynamics}

Let $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla E$ be the atomic forces and $\mathbf{v}_1$ the
eigenvector of the (mass-weighted, Eckart-projected) Hessian with the smallest
eigenvalue. GAD \cite{gad} evolves the geometry by reflecting the force about
$\mathbf{v}_1$, converting descent into ascent along that single mode while
preserving descent in every orthogonal direction:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\mathbf{x}} \;=\; \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{GAD}}
\;=\; \bigl(\mathbf{I} - 2\,\mathbf{v}_1\mathbf{v}_1^{\!\top}\bigr)\mathbf{F}
\;=\; \mathbf{F} - 2\,(\mathbf{F}\!\cdot\!\mathbf{v}_1)\,\mathbf{v}_1 .
\label{eq:gad}
\end{equation}
We integrate Eq.~\eqref{eq:gad} with a fixed-step explicit Euler scheme,
$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + dt\,\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{GAD}}(\mathbf{x}_k)$,
with $dt$ a fixed time step (we report $dt \in \{0.003, 0.005, 0.007\}$;
results are insensitive to this choice within $\sim$1--2~pp, and we use
$dt=0.005$ as the representative configuration). There is no path history, no
trust region, and no line search: each step depends only on the current
geometry. To follow the reaction mode smoothly through eigenvalue crossings,
$\mathbf{v}_1$ is chosen by maximum overlap with the previous step's mode among
the lowest few eigenvectors (mode tracking); per-atom displacements are capped
at \SI{0.35}{\AA} as a rarely triggered safety valve.

\subsection{The potential and its analytic Hessian}

All energies, forces, and Hessians come from HIP \cite{hip}, an
$SE(3)$-equivariant MLIP that returns the analytic Hessian directly
($\approx\!\SI{0.06}{s}$ per structure on these systems). HIP is not trained on
Transition1x, so the benchmark is a held-out evaluation. Because curvature is
cheap, the Hessian is recomputed exactly at \emph{every} GAD step --- the regime
in which Eq.~\eqref{eq:gad} is exact rather than approximate.

\subsection{Eckart projection and the geometry step}

A raw Cartesian Hessian mixes the six (five for linear molecules) zero-frequency
translation/rotation (TR) modes into the spectrum, contaminating both the
eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$ and the count of negative eigenvalues. We therefore
build an Eckart projector that removes the TR subspace in mass-weighted
coordinates, diagonalize the reduced $(3N\!-\!6)$ vibrational Hessian, and use
its lowest mode in Eq.~\eqref{eq:gad}. The displacement itself is taken in
Cartesian coordinates (an A/B test against a mass-weighted step changes test-set
intended success by $\le 0.35$~pp). Eckart projection is the single most
important ingredient for robustness: it ensures the dynamics climbs a genuine
vibrational mode rather than a rotation-contaminated artifact.

\subsection{Hybrid GAD--Newton}\label{sec:hybridmethod}

The hybrid walks with Eq.~\eqref{eq:gad} until the geometry is close to a clean
index-1 saddle (the vibrational spectrum shows a single negative eigenvalue and
the internal force is small), then switches to an eigenvector-following Newton
step on the same analytic Hessian, with a trust-radius cap to prevent the
$\mathbf{H}^{-1}\mathbf{F}$ blow-up near a vanishing eigenvalue. The motivation
(Section~\ref{sec:fmax}) is that the GAD step starves near the saddle while a
Newton step does not.

\subsection{Benchmark protocol}

We evaluate on 287 reactions from the Transition1x \cite{transition1x} test
split. To probe robustness to the quality of the initial guess --- the property
that matters in any real high-throughput workflow, where guesses come from
interpolation or templates rather than from the answer --- we start each
optimization from the \emph{noised true TS}: the labelled saddle geometry
perturbed by isotropic Gaussian noise of standard deviation $\sigma \in \{10,
30, 50, 100, 150, 200\}\pm$ applied to each Cartesian coordinate, following the
picometre convention used throughout the project's benchmarks. This isolates the
optimizer's basin of attraction from confounds in guess generation. As a
baseline we use Sella \cite{sella2022}, the standard open-source saddle
optimizer (iterative diagonalization + RS-P-RFO), in its strongest configuration
on this benchmark --- Cartesian coordinates with Eckart projection and an exact
analytic Hessian every step --- and, for completeness, its tuned,
internal-coordinate, and reduced-Hessian-frequency variants
(Appendix~\ref{app:sella}). All methods see the identical sample set, potential,
and convergence criterion.

\subsection{Convergence criterion (secondary)}\label{sec:conv}

We call a geometry \emph{converged} when the optimizer reaches a first-order
saddle, i.e.\ it has exactly one negative vibrational eigenvalue \emph{and} a
small residual force,
\begin{equation}
\nneg = 1 \quad\wedge\quad \fmax < \tau ,
\end{equation}
where $\fmax$ is the largest force component on any atom and $\tau$ is a force
threshold in \si{eV/\AA}. Following Sella's choice of the maximum force for
consistency with ASE optimizers \cite{sella2022}, we report a family of
thresholds (Appendix~\ref{app:crit}): the ASE default ($\tau=0.05$), the
Gaussian ``Normal'' maximum force ($\tau=0.0231$), a strict-but-reachable
project default ($\tau=0.01$), a tight threshold ($\tau=0.005$), and the value
cited as fully converged in the Sella documentation ($\tau=0.001$). Unless
stated, $\tau=0.01$. \textbf{Convergence is a necessary but insufficient
condition for a useful TS}: it certifies that a geometry is \emph{a} saddle, not
that it is the \emph{intended} one. We therefore treat convergence as a
secondary, diagnostic quantity and validate every converged candidate by the
IRC.

\subsection{IRC validation and the intended success rate (primary)}\label{sec:irc}

Our primary measure of success is whether the located TS actually connects the
intended chemistry. After optimization we follow the intrinsic reaction
coordinate forward and backward using Sella's own IRC integrator
\cite{sella2022}, and compare each IRC endpoint's molecular connectivity graph
to the labelled reactant and product by graph isomorphism (connectivity graphs
generated with Open Babel and compared with the VF2 algorithm), in the same
spirit as the recent ML-Hessian TS papers \cite{newtonnet,lmhe}. Each
optimization is classified into the standard taxonomy:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=0.05em]
\item \textbf{Intended} --- both IRC endpoints recover the intended reactant and
  product (a two-endpoint match). \emph{This is the headline ``intended success
  rate''.}
\item \textbf{Partially intended} --- exactly one endpoint matches
  (a one-endpoint match).
\item \textbf{Unintended} --- a valid saddle, but the IRC connects a different
  pair of minima.
\item \textbf{No reaction} --- the trajectory collapsed to a single minimum.
\item \textbf{TS error} --- the optimization failed to converge.
\end{itemize}

For geometric quality we additionally report the RMSD of the converged TS to the
labelled Transition1x saddle (Kabsch alignment with Hungarian atom matching).
Throughout, ``intended success rate'' is the primary axis and ``convergence
rate'' the secondary one.

The mechanism is visible in how the methods fail. Classifying every
non-converged GAD trajectory at \SI{200}{pm} by the character of its final
geometry, the failures are dominated by in-basin near-misses: of 163 failures,
71 (44\%) have drifted to a higher-order ($\nneg\!\ge\!2$) unintended saddle,
while the remaining 92 (56\%) stay at or near a correct
single-negative-eigenvalue geometry that simply did not tighten below threshold
--- the plateau orbit of Section~\ref{sec:fmax}. The Sella baseline fails the
opposite way: its Newton step lands at \emph{the nearest} saddle, which from a
poor guess is often in the wrong basin. The signature is the gap between
convergence and intended success. At \SI{200}{pm} the baseline converges
\emph{more confidently than it is right} --- its converged geometries include a
large unintended fraction that the IRC rejects, so its intended rate (23.3\%)
falls \emph{below} its convergence rate (27.2\%). GAD shows the reverse: its
intended rate (44.6\%) \emph{exceeds} its convergence rate (43.2\%), because
partially-converged geometries in the correct basin are validated by the IRC
anyway. The same asymmetry appears in geometric quality
(Section~\ref{sec:geom}): at \SI{200}{pm} the baseline develops a bimodal RMSD
distribution with a \SI{0.84}{\AA} 95th-percentile tail of unintended saddles,
while GAD and the hybrid stay unimodal and tight. Small Euler steps walk
\emph{through} basins; large Newton jumps hop \emph{between} them. When the guess
is good the distinction is invisible; when the guess is bad it is decisive.

% =====================================================================
\section{Results III: convergence and the \texorpdfstring{$\fmax$}{Fmax} plateau}\label{sec:fmax}
% =====================================================================

The same simplicity that makes GAD robust imposes a hard limit on the secondary
convergence axis, and we characterize it precisely because it motivates the
hybrid and because it is the one place a reviewer will press.

\paragraph{The plateau.} Figure~\ref{fig:plateau} plots convergence as the force
threshold $\tau$ tightens. Every method holds up well down to $\tau=0.01$. Below
that the behavior bifurcates. The Sella baseline descends smoothly, retaining
24--34\% of cases at $\tau=0.005$ (low noise) because its Newton step continues
to drive the force down. \textbf{GAD collapses to a vertical cliff: all three
$dt$ values reach 0.0\% at $\tau<0.005$ across every noise level.} Pure
first-order dynamics simply has no samples in the
\SIrange{0.001}{0.005}{eV/\AA} band.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures_new/fig_fmax_plateau.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The $\fmax$ plateau} (convergence rate vs.\ threshold
tightness; threshold tightens to the right, log scale; shaded = plateau zone).
At both \SI{50}{pm} and \SI{150}{pm} noise, GAD drops vertically near
$\fmax=0.01$, while the Sella baseline and the hybrid descend into $0.005$ via
Newton landing. No method reaches $0.001$ within the step budget.}
\label{fig:plateau}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Mechanism.} The plateau follows from Eq.~\eqref{eq:gad}. Near the
saddle the magnitude of a GAD step is
$\lVert dt\,\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{GAD}}\rVert \sim dt\,\lVert\mathbf{F}\rVert$: it
shrinks \emph{linearly} as the force shrinks. Once $\fmax$ falls to
$\approx\!0.01\evA$, the step drops below the numerical noise floor of the
soft-mode eigenvector and the trajectory orbits the saddle rather than tightening
onto it. A Newton step behaves oppositely: its size scales as
$\lVert\mathbf{H}^{-1}\mathbf{F}\rVert \sim \lVert\mathbf{F}\rVert/|\lambda|$,
which stays finite as $\mathbf{F}\to 0$. The plateau is thus structural to any
fixed-step first-order saddle dynamics; raising $dt$ trades it for instability
rather than removing it.

\paragraph{It is intrinsic, not a budget limit.} Re-running the \SI{50}{pm} cell
with a $5\times$ step budget (10{,}000 vs.\ 2{,}000 steps) targeting
$\tau=0.001$ leaves GAD's convergence at $\tau=0.01$ unchanged (85.7\% at both
budgets) and its convergence at $\tau<0.005$ \textbf{exactly 0.0\%}. Five times
the budget buys zero ground below the plateau. By contrast, Newton-based methods
given the same budget reach 47--77\% at $\tau=0.005$ (hybrid 47.2\%, $n=72$;
Sella 77.3\%, $n=66$; partial cells, SLURM timeout). The plateau is a property
of the step, not the schedule. Crucially, it lives on the \emph{secondary}
(convergence) axis: GAD's intended success (Section~\ref{sec:robust}) is
unaffected, because intended recovery does not require sub-plateau forces.

% =====================================================================
\section{Results IV: the hybrid wins cost and geometry}\label{sec:hybrid}
% =====================================================================

If the plateau is caused by the GAD step starving near the saddle, the remedy is
to use the GAD step only where it is good --- far from the saddle, where its
in-basin robustness is decisive --- and hand off to a Newton step exactly where
GAD starves (Section~\ref{sec:hybridmethod}). Because the Newton phase fires only
after GAD has placed the geometry in the correct basin, the hybrid keeps GAD's
chemistry: its intended success tracks GAD's (Table~\ref{tab:headline2}) rather
than the baseline's, and a dedicated strict Newton-polish test reaches 31.2\% at
$\tau=0.005$ and 1.2\% at $\tau=0.001$ --- the only configuration tested that
converges at the Sella documentation threshold (Appendix~\ref{app:nr}).

\section{Discussion and conclusion}
% =====================================================================

The conventional reading of TS optimization is that more curvature information,
applied through a more sophisticated quasi-Newton step, yields a better
optimizer. Our results invert that reading in the regime MLIPs have made common.
When the analytic Hessian is cheap, the limiting factor is not how much curvature
you have but how you spend it, and the sophisticated step --- the large
trust-region Newton jump --- is precisely what fails when the initial guess is
poor, because it jumps across basin boundaries and converges, with full
confidence, to an unintended saddle. A deliberately minimal first-order GAD walk
avoids that failure by construction: it is more robust on the metric that
matters (+21.3~pp intended success at \SI{200}{pm}), ties at low noise, and ---
in its hybrid form --- is faster and geometrically tighter than the standard
baseline at every noise level.
% TODO: same correction as Results II — no unintended saddles; recast as
% recover-both-channels vs partial/non-convergence; cite the trend not 200pm.

We have also been explicit about the cost of simplicity. First-order dynamics
cannot tighten the maximum force below a plateau near $\approx\!0.01\evA$, and no
step budget removes it. But this plateau lives on the secondary convergence axis,
not the primary intended-success axis, and the hybrid removes it where it matters
by switching to a Newton step exactly in the regime where GAD starves ---
recovering sub-plateau convergence while keeping GAD's in-basin robustness,
because the switch happens only after the geometry is already in the right basin.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.} For high-throughput reaction discovery, where
initial guesses are imperfect and the chemically intended TS is what matters, the
GAD family is the more reliable choice, and the Hybrid GAD--Newton optimizer is
the recommended default: it is the fastest method on this benchmark, the most
chemically reliable at high noise, and the most geometrically precise.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The benchmark perturbs the true saddle to isolate the
optimizer's basin of attraction; guesses from interpolation or templates carry
structured rather than isotropic error, and confirming the same ranking there is
future work. Convergence below $\tau=0.001$ remains out of reach for all methods
on this surface within practical budgets. Finally, the hybrid's Newton trigger is
gated only on a single-negative-eigenvalue test; gating additionally on
eigenvector continuity is expected to further reduce the residual unintended
rate.

\section{Sella baseline configurations}\label{app:sella}

The Sella baseline is specified on three axes:
\textbf{Sella \{cartesian $|$ internal\} \{Eckart\}? \{tuned $|$ untuned\}
Hess.Freq.=$N$}, where \emph{tuned} sets trust-radius hyperparameters

and Hess.Freq.=$N$ recomputes the Hessian every $N$ steps (library default 3;
project canonical 1). The strongest configuration on this benchmark, used as the
headline baseline, is \emph{Sella cartesian Eckart untuned Hess.Freq.=1}.

\paragraph{A cautionary tale: more convergence, worse chemistry.} Recomputing
the Hessian only every third step (Hess.Freq.=3, the library default)
\emph{raises} the convergence rate at low noise --- 96.5\% vs.\ 92.7\% at
\SI{10}{pm}, at identical wall-time --- because the cheaper, smoother
intermediate steps overshoot the strict threshold less often. But the intended
success rate reverses the verdict at every noise level (Table~\ref{tab:d1d3}):
the stale Hessian misjudges saddle character, so the extra ``convergences'' are
unintended saddles the IRC rejects. This is a compact illustration of the
paper's theme --- convergence is not chemistry --- and it justifies the
every-step (d=1) baseline.

\section{Newton-polish: reaching below the plateau}\label{app:nr}

To confirm that Newton stepping --- not GAD --- reaches sub-plateau forces, we
ran a strict Newton-Raphson polish (spectral-partitioned NR with a trust-radius
cap, \texttt{nr\_max\_step\_norm}=0.3) at \SI{50}{pm}, $n=80$, against a loose
variant that stops at $\tau=0.01$.

\begin{table}[h]

at the canonical $\tau=0.01$, which is why the headline hybrid uses the
non-overshooting switch.
